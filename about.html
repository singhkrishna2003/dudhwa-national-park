The Legacy of Dudhwa

Dudhwa National Park stands as a testament to India's commitment to wildlife conservation. Located in the Lakhimpur Kheri district of Uttar Pradesh, this remarkable reserve protects one of the last remaining Terai ecosystems in the Indian subcontinent.

A Brief History

The journey of Dudhwa began in 1958 when the area was designated as a wildlife sanctuary to protect the endangered swamp deer population. The transformation into a national park came in 1977, largely due to the tireless efforts of legendary conservationist Billy Arjan Singh, who championed the cause before Prime Minister Indira Gandhi.

Billy Arjan Singh, often called the "Tiger Man of India", made history by successfully hand-rearing and releasing zoo-born tigers into the wild at Dudhwa. His groundbreaking work proved that captive-bred big cats could thrive in natural habitats, setting a precedent for conservation worldwide.

In 1987, Dudhwa achieved another milestone when it was declared a tiger reserve under Project Tiger. Today, along with Kishanpur and Katarniaghat sanctuaries, it forms the Dudhwa Tiger Reserve, covering approximately 1,284 square kilometers.

The Rhino Revival

One of Dudhwa's most celebrated achievements is the successful reintroduction of the Indian one-horned rhinoceros. These magnificent creatures once roamed the region but were hunted to extinction nearly 150 years ago.

In 1984–85, seven rhinos were relocated from Assam and Nepal, marking the beginning of an ambitious restoration project. Today, the population has grown to over 46 individuals, thriving within dedicated conservation areas.

Geography and Ecosystem

Dudhwa occupies the fertile Indo-Gangetic plains, characterized by vast alluvial terrain interspersed with the Mohana and Suheli rivers. The landscape features dense sal forests, expansive grasslands, seasonal wetlands, and numerous streams that create ideal habitats for diverse wildlife.

The park’s unique Terai-Bhabar terrain supports some of the finest sal tree forests in the world, alongside species like jamun, shisham, and gular. Approximately 19% of the park comprises grasslands, serving as crucial grazing zones for herbivores.

Wildlife at Dudhwa

Mammals

Bengal tiger, Indian rhinoceros, Asian elephant, leopard, sloth bear, swamp deer (Barasingha), spotted deer, sambar, hog deer, barking deer, hispid hare, fishing cat, jungle cat, and more.

Birds

Sarus crane, Bengal florican, great hornbill, painted stork, bar-headed goose, kingfishers, woodpeckers, owls, and over 400 bird species.

Endangered Species

Dudhwa has become a sanctuary for critically endangered species such as the Bengal florican and hispid hare, both rediscovered here after being thought extinct.

Conservation Impact

Dudhwa plays a vital role in maintaining ecological balance in the region. Conservation programs have led to remarkable increases in wildlife populations.

Barasingha numbers grew from 3,691 in 1977 to over 6,137 in 2022, while leopard populations increased from 34 to 93 in recent years.
